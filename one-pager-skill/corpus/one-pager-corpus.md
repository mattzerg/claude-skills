# One-Pager Corpus (10 docs)

Per-doc breakdown + synthesis. Used as ground truth for "X doc does this well" exemplar references in scaffold and review output. Pulled from Matt's Google Drive (matteisn@gmail.com), curated 2026-05-05.

The corpus splits into three buckets that map directly to the three variants:

- **Product/company sell sheets** (6): Hoy Health B2B, Hoy Health B2C, Joi, Econometrics, Intercept TeleMed, eHubCo, RELAYTO
- **Services briefs** (3): Algorand Ecosystem Growth, Quit Genius Growth, Pento Growth & Analytics — all Matt-authored at Vang
- **Hybrid** (1): RELAYTO (lives in product but its 7Ms framing is reusable for company variants)

## Product / Company sell sheets

### 1. Hoy Health B2B
**Drive:** `1NTpQbwMq1jWgS6jod4R-tnTS3Gtel1By` · PDF
**Audience:** Enterprise / institutional buyer (employer, payer, plan).
**Variant fit:** company, product

**What it does well:**
- Headline is a category claim ("affordable, integrated healthcare for underserved Hispanic populations"), not a buzzword.
- Lead paragraph compresses mission + market + product into ~40 words.
- Product list section is a clean 3-column grid: B2B suite (HoyMEDS, HoyRX, HoyLIFE) with one-line each.
- Contact line is a real email + URL, not "get in touch."

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Slight overuse of social-impact language ("we believe everyone deserves…") — reads warm but takes a sentence away from proof.

**Quote-ready hook:** "Affordable, integrated healthcare for underserved populations" — declarative, specific population, no buzzword.

### 2. Hoy Health B2C
**Drive:** `14Ew0RMev02lfvaU4G9dcveTdWln_Hoxr` · PDF
**Audience:** Consumer.
**Variant fit:** product

**What it does well:**
- Same skeleton as B2B variant, but the headline + lead reorders the value props for a different audience. **The B2B/B2C dual is the cleanest case study in the corpus for "same skeleton, different audience."**
- Product list mirrors B2B (HoyMEDS, HoyRX, HoyLIFE) but the descriptions emphasize end-user experience, not procurement.
- Pricing is implicit (consumer-friendly, e.g., "no insurance needed").

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- The B2C variant could surface a price point ("from $X/month") and would be stronger.

**Quote-ready hook:** Same product line, different framing — proves that audience targeting beats writing one universal piece.

### 3. Joi
**Drive:** `1MD9fOIZCSO-OgJ8tIyKaoGHQmU31hRrG` · PDF
**Audience:** Investors (seed round).
**Variant fit:** product (investor variant)

**What it does well:**
- Investor-shape one-pager. Beat sequence: Overview → Problem & Solution → Team → Target Market → Revenue Model → Investment ($125k seed).
- Specific number anchors: ask amount, traction metric.
- Team section is dense (founder names + prior companies + role).

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- "AI Bot for direct sales" is generic — the one-pager doesn't make a strong differentiator claim against incumbents.
- Revenue model is sketchy (typical for seed); the doc would be stronger with a single anchor metric vs. projection.

**Quote-ready hook:** "$125k seed, [traction signal], converting [%] of leads" — investor one-pagers live or die on one-line metrics.

### 4. Econometrics
**Drive:** `1JKtlv0HpLvY0H8w-qxZjuAmUhcgpfANViYEbn4L-OYg` · Google Doc (shared)
**Audience:** Privacy-tech buyer / partner.
**Variant fit:** product

- The team section runs long — could be cut to leave room for a stronger CTA.

### 5. Intercept TeleMed
**Drive:** `1bwQKDO8IHCR01y7wDIsat32ezoK_Cn52` · PDF (Gust template)
**Audience:** Investors / VCs.
**Variant fit:** product (investor variant)

**What it does well:**
- Gust standard structure: Company Summary → Mgmt → Customer Problem → Product/Services → Target Market → Business Model → Competitors → Competitive Advantage → Financials (5yr).
- Each section is a tight paragraph (~40 words). Forces discipline.
- 5-year financials grid at the bottom is the canonical investor-credibility move.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Templated structure can read as bureaucratic if every section is filled equally. Don't pad weak sections to match strong ones.

**Quote-ready hook:** The "Competitors" + "Competitive Advantage" pair forces the differentiation claim into a separate beat — reusable pattern for any one-pager with real competitive density.

### 6. RELAYTO
**Drive:** `1QjGvB1J52x5SB8zpCqiHW_bLDTQ4evwJ_k43pkndLiE` · Google Doc
**Audience:** Enterprise SaaS buyer.
**Variant fit:** company, product

**What it does well:**
- **The 7Ms framing.** WHAT'S IN IT FOR [CLIENT] / WHY NOW / WHAT / WHERE TO USE / WHEN / HOW / WHO → PROOF POINTS / RECOGNITION / INTEGRATIONS / CONTACT. The most reusable structural move for company-variant one-pagers where the audience is genuinely heterogeneous.
- "WHAT'S IN IT FOR [CLIENT]" forces the doc to lead with reader value, not company history.
- Recognition (awards / press) is its own beat, not buried in a footer.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- 11+ section headers can fragment a one-pager. RELAYTO works because each section is 1-3 lines; if any section bloats, the structure breaks.

**Quote-ready hook:** "WHAT'S IN IT FOR [CLIENT]" as opening beat — pull this into the Zerg company one-pager (network leave-behind variant).

### 7. eHubCo
**Drive:** `1Hg85rC7hXG8uyHp0o3-lgzLbdA2BK-4o` · PDF
**Audience:** Mixed (e-commerce / global retailers).
**Variant fit:** company

**What it does well:**
- Headline + 3-4 bullet value props + logo/partnership carousel + growth strategy callout. Very visual.
- Logos serve as both proof AND credibility-by-association without quote boxes.
- The "growth strategy" beat at the bottom is unusual — most one-pagers stop at proof. This adds forward-looking credibility.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Heavy visual reliance — without the logos, the prose alone underdelivers. Don't let visuals do all the work.

**Quote-ready hook:** Logo grids as proof are a deck-style move that translates to one-pagers when you have 6+ named clients. For Zerg with ~3-5, inline names are stronger.

## Services briefs (Matt-authored at Vang)

### 8. Algorand Ecosystem Growth Brief
**Drive:** `1yfdTp0T_fD12lvqlPqfl8veHeQPp8aP969weasFVh0k` · Google Doc
**Audience:** Algorand foundation (services prospect).
**Variant fit:** consulting

**What it does well:**
- **The canonical reference shape for Zerg Solutions one-pagers.** Beat sequence: Overview → Scopes of Work (7 modules w/ pricing) → Admin (pricing structure) → About Us (bios + cases).
- 7 workstreams are categories of engagement, not a service menu. Each has a 1-2 sentence scope hint.
- Pricing transparency: explicit day rate and per-module project pricing. **The strongest move in the corpus** — most consulting one-pagers hide pricing; surfacing it is a trust signal.
- "About Us" section is named bios with prior companies + relevant cases.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- 7 workstreams is at the upper edge — for shorter engagements, 3-5 is tighter.

**Quote-ready hook:** The workstream + day-rate format. Reuse verbatim shape for Zerg Solutions, just swap content. **This is the model.**

### 9. Quit Genius Growth Brief
**Drive:** `1kb5wBlykHgrvdgXSUGYv78sqroaWZpCoBFRobN07fjk` · Google Doc (Matt, 2017)
**Audience:** Quit Genius (digital health, services prospect).
**Variant fit:** consulting

**What it does well:**
- Beat sequence: Overview → Potential Scope of Work (5 workstream buckets) → Potential Intros (network list) → About Matthew.
- **The "Potential Intros" beat is rare and powerful.** Surfaces the network value of the engagement separate from the workstreams. For Zerg Solutions, this could surface as "warm intros into Y community / X partner ecosystem" if the engagement includes that.
- "About Matthew" is single-principal but reads strong — names prior companies + specific competence.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- "Potential" framing on every section can read as tentative. For Zerg's stage, prefer declarative ("We deliver" over "Potential scope").

**Quote-ready hook:** "Most recently with [Companies], where Matt [specific scope]" — the past-work line that grounds bios in real work.

**Drive:** `14NJiiMAyropfN2YdVk5NGiQu43kfOnpt-8PYujbcc14` · Google Doc (Matt/Ansible)
**Audience:** Pento (services prospect).

## Synthesis — recurring patterns

### What 8+/10 docs do

| Pattern | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Headline is a complete claim, not a category label | 9/10 | Exception: eHubCo (logo-led) |
| Strongest proof in top third | 8/10 | Joi and Pento bury team credibility late |
| Single primary CTA with real destination | 9/10 | RELAYTO has multiple ("HOW") but each routes to the same primary action |
| Named clients / past work | 9/10 | Investor variants use traction metrics in this slot |
| Differentiation paragraph (vs. just description) | 7/10 | Joi and eHubCo lean on category alone — weakness |
| Pricing or engagement-model line | 8/10 | Hoy Health B2C is implicit; Algorand/Pento explicit; weakest are the investor pitches that hide it |
| First-person plural ("we") for company/consulting | 10/10 | Universal |
| 3-5 sections of body content (not 7+) | 8/10 | RELAYTO 7Ms works because each section is tight; if you can't keep sections to 1-3 lines, drop count |

### What changes between B2B and B2C variants (Hoy Health study)

| Element | B2B | B2C |
|---|---|---|
| Headline emphasis | Population served, integration claim | End-user benefit, ease |
| Product descriptions | Procurement / integration language | Outcome / "what you get" |
| Pricing | Implicit ("contact us for plans") | Implicit but cheaper-feel ("no insurance needed") |
| Proof | Named institutional partners | Population reach numbers |
| CTA | Contact sales | Sign up / try free |

**Lesson:** ship two one-pagers, not one. Same skeleton, different beats reordered, different language register.

### What separates a strong consulting one-pager from a deck

The Vang briefs (Algorand, Quit Genius, Pento) all do the same three things deck slides don't:

1. **Pricing transparency.** Day rate, project minimum, or workstream estimate. The deck can leave this open; the one-pager must commit.
2. **Workstream categories with scope hints.** Not a service list ("we do X, Y, Z") but engagement categories ("Workstream 1: [name]. [What this engagement looks like in 1-2 sentences]").
3. **Single primary CTA.** A scoping call. Not "let's chat" — a calendly URL.

If a Zerg Solutions one-pager doesn't do these three, it's a deck slide pretending.

### What separates a strong company one-pager from a product one-pager

| Dimension | Company variant | Product variant |
|---|---|---|
| Hero claim | What the org does (across products) | What the product does (specifically) |
| Body | "What we ship" + "Why us" + proof | Capabilities + pricing + integration |
| Pricing | Often implicit (link to product pricing pages) | Always explicit (tier table) |
| CTA | Contact / book a call | Sign up / try free |
| Audience | Multi (sales, partners, network) | Single (product evaluator) |

Watch for **mixed-shape drafts** — a "company" one-pager that turns into a product spec sheet weakens both.

## Anti-pattern frequency

These appeared across 3+ corpus docs and should be reviewed for in any draft:

1. **Mission-y opening.** "We believe…" / "Our mission is…" — 4/10 lead with this. Strongest docs (Algorand, Pento) don't.
2. **Buzzword stack.** "transformative," "next-generation," "comprehensive" appear in 5/10 — surface as one rolled-up finding.
3. **Generic "leading companies."** 3/10 use this in proof slots where a real client name would fit. Always cut.
4. **Pricing hidden behind "contact us" with no rationale.** 5/10 do this — half are correctly gated, half are weakness.
5. **Multiple competing CTAs.** 2/10 — when present, always weakens.

## How to use this corpus in scaffolds and reviews

- **Scaffold:** cite specific corpus doc patterns inline as italicized notes (e.g., "*Algorand brief uses this 4-row table — feel free to reuse.*"). Don't over-cite — one exemplar per beat at most.
- **Review:** when flagging a finding, name the corpus doc that does it well or demonstrates the anti-pattern. "Hoy Health B2C surfaces pricing implicitly via 'no insurance needed' — your draft hides pricing without that signal."
- **Calibration:** if a draft does X and 8/10 corpus docs do X, that's a "calibrated" choice — don't flag. If a draft does X and 8/10 corpus docs do the opposite, that's HIGH confidence the draft should change.
